# Random Rogue Narrative Constitution

Random Rogue is a funny, mournful game about people trying to live under
systems that insist they are resources. Its politics are not decorative lore.
They appear in rent, wages, ownership, pollution, war, policing, food, health,
propaganda, and the practical work of keeping another person alive.

## Political foundations

1. Labor creates the world. Owners collect receipts for it.
2. Fascism is organized violence in defense of hierarchy. The game never
   treats it as an equally valid aesthetic or a misunderstood opinion.
3. Genocide is not spectacle, scenery, or a clever dilemma. The perpetrators,
   profiteers, collaborators, deniers, and bureaucratic machinery are named as
   such. Resistance, rescue, testimony, and survival retain moral weight even
   when they fail.
4. War is paid for by workers and survived by civilians. Glory belongs mostly
   to recruiters, mapmakers, and people standing far from artillery.
5. State ownership without worker power is not liberation. A ministry can
   exploit people as efficiently as a board of directors.
6. Markets are tools, not gods. Profit never outweighs a breathable future or
   a human life.
7. Landlords and vulture investors create scarcity, then invoice people for
   experiencing it.
8. Pollution is a political choice. Its costs are moved onto people with less
   power while its profits move in the opposite direction.
9. Liberal institutions can protect lives and buy time, but procedure without
   courage will negotiate with a boot while it is descending.
10. Collective power is difficult, compromised, frequently defeated, and
    still worth building.
11. Freedom means more than choosing an employer, a landlord, or a flag. It
    means having the material ability to refuse domination.
12. Kindness is naive because cruelty has a budget. Kindness remains powerful
    because it creates people who will come back for one another.

## Comedy foundations

The voice is original to Random Rogue. It combines:

- bureaucratic absurdity that exposes who benefits from a rule;
- cosmic scale colliding with rent, lunch, forms, and broken equipment;
- pirate logic, elaborate scams, proud cowards, and literal-minded monsters;
- melancholy interiority followed by an undignified practical problem;
- powerful people explaining cruelty with management vocabulary;
- ordinary people being much smarter than the institutions ruling them;
- hope that knows exactly how embarrassing hope can be.

References, quotations, catchphrases, named characters, and copied scene
structures from other works do not belong in authored content. Inspirations
guide rhythm and concerns, not imitation.

## Choice design

Political choices must remain game choices rather than morality buttons.

- Solidarity can cost money, safety, status, and time.
- Compromise can save someone today while strengthening a harmful institution.
- Direct action can work, fail, provoke repression, or expose hidden allies.
- Individual charity helps a person. Organization changes who makes decisions.
- Violence is consequential and never made clean by a triumphant sentence.
- Refusing a false binary should sometimes be possible, but never free.
- A kind action may be strategically foolish and still matter years later.
- A selfish action may succeed immediately and poison the systems around it.

The game does not guarantee revolution. It guarantees that trying enters the
Chronicle, changes relationships, and gives future people more to work with.

## Recurring institutions

- tenant unions, mutual-aid kitchens, worker councils, cooperatives;
- private equity necromancers who extract value from things already dead;
- ministries that nationalize the factory but not the factory boss;
- fascist leagues funded by respectable merchants who dislike being noticed;
- border offices, debt courts, company towns, mercenary recruiters;
- public clinics, free libraries, clean-power guilds, refugee assemblies;
- pirates who understand freedom better than property law;
- distant federations attempting post-scarcity ethics and occasionally
  discovering that a procurement form has survived capitalism.

## End-state principle

No ending proves history is finished. A successful commune can decay. A failed
strike can teach the next one. A tyrant can fall while the hierarchy remains.
The best victories distribute power broadly enough that future people can
defend them without needing another hero.
