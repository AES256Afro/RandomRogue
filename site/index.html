Release 18: living politics

Before your first step, the recipe runs: one thousand years of geography, factions, wars, labor, property, artifacts, named beasts, plagues, and a century when things wouldn't stop falling out of the sky. Five ages. Five spoken tongues: goblins sound goblin, swamp folk sound damp, dead corporations sound like liability waivers. Cities burn and become the dungeons you'll loot. Then you're born into the wreckage with six stats, a rusty sword, and, if you choose one, an ambition. Ten world conditions, ten institutions, regional politics, and named agendas keep moving after you arrive.

A Story Director deals the cards

Exactly 1,000 scenarios, visible dice rolls, and hundreds of conditional twists. All 351 legacy cards carry material stakes. Fifty-nine new eight-beat families return after time and remember who gained power. Exact cards never repeat in one life, and the world cools its last 240 cards across generations.

Choices schedule consequences

Mercy, theft, threats, and favors can return days later. Some cards begin multi-part stories. The Story Ledger tracks open work without revealing the surprise waiting at the end.

People remember what you did

Named people track trust, fear, respect, debt, affection, knowledge, and grudges. They also have families, rivals, employers, proteges, debtors, and confidants of their own. Helping one person can move an entire web.

Mysteries know the answer first

Every run can hide a generated culprit, victim, site, and artifact. The truth is fixed before the first clue appears. Gather evidence, weigh doubt, accuse a suspect, and live with a verdict that can be wrong.

Pin the case to a board

The Investigation Board compares suspects, motives, alibis, testimony reliability, evidence, and doubt. Mark either suspect, preview the case's strength, and appeal a failed verdict instead of pretending it never happened.

Follow the lines between people

The People viewer turns remembered NPCs into a navigable social network. Inspect opinions, debts, grudges, employers, relatives, rivals, and hidden connections that become visible as your knowledge grows.

Work is procedurally complicated

Contracts can ask you to hunt, deliver, reconcile, investigate, survey, recover an artifact, or stock a mutual-aid table. Collective work changes the region instead of merely changing your wallet.

Regions change around you

Prosperity, danger, unrest, rent, supply, pollution, and solidarity move over time. Tenant unions, public clinics, company towns, clean power, refugees, and wars change prices and pressure neighboring regions.

The whole world has material conditions

Worker power, rent burden, concentrated wealth, pollution, militarization, food security, fascist influence, solidarity, legitimacy, and mutual aid react to choices and autonomous history. None of them is a disguised morality score.

Institutions can betray their purpose

Unions, tenant councils, cooperatives, peace movements, public labs, restoration crews, pirate assemblies, and revolutionary committees can grow, bureaucratize, lose integrity, collapse, and be rebuilt.

Power keeps making plans

Named people organize workers, build mutual aid, expose crimes, displace tenants, pollute land, militarize streets, and profit from scarcity. Their agendas advance even when you are somewhere else.

Rumors travel before consequences

Reports spread between regions, mutate with distance, and sometimes warn you about a coming crisis. Verify, amplify, or bury them, then decide who benefits from what everyone believes.

Solidarity is powerful, not free

Strikes, rescues, tenant defense, anti-fascist resistance, clean power, public care, and common ownership ask for risk and sacrifice. Kindness is allowed to be naive. It is also allowed to win.

The thousand-scenario map is complete

The playable deck now contains exactly 1,000 ids across twenty political and human themes. Structural fingerprints cool repeated openings, joke engines, social roles, and choice shapes even when the card id is new.

The jokes have material conditions

A demon's mother brings it soup. Dungeon monsters organize a union. A ministry denies its own existence on official stationery. Vulture investors auction a cooperative at its funeral. Absurdity points at power, never at the people surviving it.

Your pack is a workbench

Combine useful junk into reinforced satchels, honeyed rations, field antiseptic, warded rope, evidence dossiers, surveyor kits, monster bait, charms, and one engine that should not understand probability.

Story endings escape their borders

Finish an inheritance, refugee crisis, trade boom, false saint case, guild schism, or walking-town dispute and it keeps changing ordinary life. Survivors become vendors and apprentices. Prices shift, thieves arrive, artifacts move, and later cards inherit the damage.

The Director can explain itself

Open diagnostics from Options or press F8 to see card score, active arc priority, family heat, context bonuses, recent structures, and underused tags. It reveals why a card was selected without exposing outcomes or touching the dice.

Private by design

Optional anonymous tuning sends only aggregate card, choice, deck, day band, repetition gap, and Director score counters. It sends no name, seed, free text, or individual play record, and it can be disabled in Options.

Readable on more screens

Music remains muted by default. Reader text can be enlarged, controls can use high-contrast borders, long scenario and ledger entries scroll, and a backup autosave can recover the previous valid turn after a damaged save.

The Chronicle absorbs you

Die, and you become history: your epitaph enters the record, your relics scatter into future runs, your heir carries the name, and your ghost waits where you fell. Player-caused history is marked in the Chronicle.

Shared worlds collect the dead

Daily and weekly worlds turn other players' deaths into fresh graves. Read their epitaphs, loot their relics, hear their deeds as news, and replay their final journey card by card.

A run becomes a story

Random Rogue does not only ask what happens next. It remembers why it happened, who saw it, and what the world should do about it.

Below the world: the MUD

The Wide World has a basement. Random Rogue MUD is a free multiplayer text dungeon running beneath this game: the same seed-verse, the same gods of doors and debts, twenty-five floors of it, live and shared with other players. Play it in the browser, install the Rogue Deck app, or point any telnet MUD client at it.

The two games keep one ledger. When your run ends up here, your character crosses over: the fallen walk the MUD's halls as ghosts, telling strangers who they were, how many days they lasted, and what their epitaph said. Dungeon crawlers can examine your ghost and learn from your mistakes, which is more than most of us get. Their dead are listed on the MUD's Wall of the Fallen; yours will be too.
